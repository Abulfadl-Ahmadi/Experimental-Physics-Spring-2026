### 2.3 Lasers and LEDs

**Slide 8 (Other Light Sources: Physics Principles of Laser and LED)**
- **Persian text translated:**
  - Other light sources: Physics principles of laser and LED
  - **Properties of laser light:**
    1. It is coherent (all beams are in phase).
    2. It is intense: because it is coherent there is no destructive interference of the various components of the beam.
    3. It is monochromatic, to the extent described.
- **Images and Diagrams:**
  - **Right side:** Three-step diagram for stimulated emission:
    1. **Before emission:** Atom in excited level \(E_2\) and an incident photon with energy \(h\nu\).
    2. **During emission:** Incident photon stimulates the atom, which begins to emit.
    3. **After emission:** Atom returns to ground level \(E_1\) and two photons with energy \(h\nu\) are emitted.
  - **Formulas and Physical Equations (right side):** \(E_2 - E_1 = \Delta E = h\nu\)
  - **Left side:** An animation of a laser labeled "Animation explaining stimulated emission and the laser principle" with a Wikipedia link https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser at the bottom.
- **Bottom text box (laser operation principle, translated):**
  - Assume a two-level system. At thermal equilibrium, all atoms/electrons are in energy level \(E_1\). To achieve population inversion, atoms must be moved from \(E_1\) to \(E_2\), e.g., by shining light or electrical energy. If an atom goes from \(E_2\) to \(E_1\), energy \(E_2 - E_1\) is released, which can be as a photon (as in laser and LED) or as heat (ordinary diodes). The transition from \(E_2\) to \(E_1\) occurs in two ways:
    - 1- Spontaneous emission (application in LED)
    - 2- Stimulated emission (application in laser)
    - Population inversion is the basis of laser and LED operation.

**Slide 9 (Laser and What It Is)**
- **Persian text translated (top box):**
  - **LASER**
  - A laser is a device that emits light through a process of optical amplification based on "light amplification by stimulated emission of radiation".
  - The word "laser" is an acronym for "Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation".
- **Middle image:** A working helium-neon (He-Ne) laser emitting a red beam.
- **Red box text (translated):**
  - The pump is an external energy source that creates **population inversion** in the laser medium. Amplification of light waves by the radiation field only occurs in a laser medium where population inversion between two **energy levels** exists.
  - For the laser to work, the number of atoms in energy level 2 must be greater than in energy level 1. This is called population inversion. Population inversion and stimulated emission work together in the laser medium to amplify light. Otherwise, light passing through the laser medium will be attenuated. Pumps can be optical, electrical, chemical, or thermal, as long as they provide the necessary energy to excite atoms and create population inversion.
- **Image (bottom left):** Structure of a helium-neon laser (Helium-neon gas reservoir) including Anode, Cathode, Laser bore tube, Output coupler, High reflector.

**Slide 10 (Helium-Neon Laser)**
- **Persian text translated:**
  - **Helium-Neon Laser:** A gas laser operating at 632.8 nm, in the red part of the visible spectrum! It operates continuously (Continuous Wave or CW), meaning its output is uninterrupted, unlike pulsed lasers.
- **Text box (main components of He-Ne laser, translated):**
  - **1. Electrical discharge tube:** Usually made of glass or quartz, depending on required power. Inside, a mixture of helium and neon gas at very low pressure (about 1 to 10 torr). Low pressure is needed for electrical discharge and excitation of atoms.
  - **2. Electrodes:** Two metal electrodes, one cathode (negative) and one anode (positive), at the ends of the tube. Connected to a power supply, they pass current through the gas, initiating the excitation process.
  - **3. Power supply:** A high-voltage source (typically 1000–2000 volts) is required for electrical discharge. Must provide constant, controlled current.
  - **4. Optical resonator:** Two mirrors at the ends of the tube. The first mirror, fully reflective (100% reflection), returns light into the cavity. The second, partially reflective (usually 98–99%), lets some light out as the usable laser beam. The precise distance between mirrors is tuned to amplify a specific wavelength.
  - **5. Active gases:** A mixture of helium and neon in roughly a 10:1 ratio. Helium acts as an energy transfer agent, while neon atoms produce the laser light. This optimized mixture ensures efficient laser operation.
- **Graphs and Diagrams:**
  - Three small images of solid-state laser structures including a helical flash lamp and cylindrical reflectors surrounding the laser crystal.

**Slide 11 (Mechanism of He-Ne Gas Laser)**
- **Persian text translated (right side):**
  - **Mechanism of He-Ne Gas Laser**
  - Basic principles involve three steps:
  - **Energy pumping:** When electric current passes through the discharge tube, electrons collide with helium atoms, raising them from ground state to a higher energy level. This requires significant electrical energy; the power supply is crucial here.
  - **Energy transfer:** Excited helium atoms cannot produce laser light alone, but their excited energy level aligns with neon energy levels. Through atomic collisions, energy transfers from helium to neon, exciting neon atoms. This transfer is very efficient and the main reason for using the He-Ne mixture in this laser.
  - **Stimulated emission and amplification:** Excited neon atoms, returning to a lower energy level, emit photons at specific wavelengths like 632.8 nm. These photons reflect back and forth in the optical resonator between mirrors, stimulating other neon atoms to emit more in-phase photons. This process creates intense, monochromatic, coherent light, a hallmark of lasers.
- **Graph and Diagram (left side):**
  - **Energy level diagram** for Helium (He) and Neon (Ne).
  - Vertical axis: Energy.
  - **Helium:** Electrons go to excited states \(2^3S_1\) and \(2^1S_1\) via electron impact (pumping). Then energy transfer to Neon (He-Ne collision).
  - **Neon:** Higher energy levels like \(5s\) and \(4s\) (aligned with He) and levels \(3s\), \(4p\), \(3p\).
  - Laser transitions include \(3.39 \mu m\), \(1.15 \mu m\), and especially **\(632.8 nm\)** (red line) from \(3s_2\) to \(3p_4\). "Fast radiative transitions" and "Diffusion to walls" (to de-excite atoms to ground state) are shown.
- **Chemical/Physical formulas:**
  - \(He^*(2^3S_1) + Ne^1S_0 \rightarrow He^1S_0 + Ne^*4s_2 + \Delta E\)
  - \(He^*(2^1S_1) + Ne^1S_0 + \Delta E \rightarrow He^1S_0 + Ne^*5s_2\)

### 5.1 Photon Detectors

**Slide 32 (Photon Detectors and Photoelectric Effect)**
- **Persian text translated:**

- **Bottom right text box (specifications of optical detectors):**
  - **What is Photo Detectors?**
  - • Optical receivers convert optical signal (light) to electrical signal (current/voltage) – Hence referred ‘O/E Converter’
  - • Photodetector is the fundamental element of optical receiver, followed by amplifiers and signal conditioning circuitry
  - • There are several photodetector types: – Photodiodes, Phototransistors, Photon multipliers, Photo-resistors etc.
- **Requirements list:**
  - • Good sensitivity (Responsivity) at the desired wavelength and poor Responsivity elsewhere -> **wavelength selectivity**.
  - • Fast response time -> **high bandwidth**.
  - • Compatible physical dimensions.
  - • Low noise.
  - • Insensitive to temperature variations.
  - • Long operating life and reasonable cost.

**Slide 33 (Photoresistive Detectors and Semiconductor Principles)**
- **Persian text translated (top):**
  - Photoresistive detectors: the best materials are semiconductors; upon absorbing light, the number of charge carriers increases and resistance changes. The resistance change depends on light intensity.
- **Left diagram (working principle):**
  - Energy band diagram for a semiconductor (Valence Band VB, Conduction Band CB). A photon with energy greater than bandgap (\(h\nu > Eg\)) excites an electron from VB to CB, creating a hole and increasing conductivity.
- **Formulas and Physical Equations (with red handwritten marks):**
  - Total conductivity: \(\sigma = \sigma_0 + e\Delta P(\mu_n + \mu_p)\), \(\Delta n = \Delta p\)
  - Relative conductivity change: \(\frac{\Delta \sigma}{\sigma_0} = \frac{\Delta n \mu_n(1+b)}{\sigma_0}\), \(b = \frac{\mu_p}{\mu_n}\) (hole to electron mobility ratio)
  - Relation with incident light intensity \(I(\nu)\): \(\frac{\Delta \sigma}{\sigma_0} = \frac{a I(\nu) \tau \mu_n (1+b)}{h \sigma_0}\)
  - Also \(\Delta n = \frac{a I(\nu) \tau}{h \nu}\) (change in carrier concentration)
  - Generated photocurrent: \(I_{ph} = \frac{A}{d} \sigma V\) (considering area \(A\), thickness \(d\), applied voltage \(V\))

## 6. Important Factors and Parameters in Detection

**Slide 40 (Important Factors and Parameters in Detection)**
- **Persian text translated:**
  - **Important factors and parameters in detection**
  - ✅ 1- Sensitivity: signal-to-noise ratio, ability to detect minimum and maximum energy.
  - ✅ 2- Response speed: depending on mechanism, can be from nanoseconds to minutes. For example, thermal detectors are slow, photoelectric are fast.
  - ✅ 3- Response range: frequency or wavelength.
  - ✅ 4- Selectivity: distinguishing the target from environmental factors.
  - ✅ 5- Cost: depends on materials, fabrication, packaging, and sensor lifetime.
  - ✅ 6- Environmental safety: for humans, soil, water, and air.
