# Atomic and Molecular Spectra

## Atomic Spectra
Light sources are divided into **continuous** (like [[blackbody-radiation]]) and **discrete** (like elemental gas discharges). 
Discrete spectra arise from quantum systems like atoms, where electrons occupy quantized energy levels. 

### Hydrogen Spectrum
The simplest atom. Energy levels are given by $\mathcal{E} = -2\pi\hbar c R / n^2$.
Transitions between levels ($n_1$ and $n_2$) emit or absorb specific photons. The series are categorized by their lower energy level:
- **Lyman Series** ($n_1=1$): Far UV (e.g., 121.5 nm)
- **Balmer Series** ($n_1=2$): Near UV and Visible (e.g., $H\alpha$ at 656 nm)
- **Paschen Series** ($n_1=3$): Near IR
- **Brackett & Pfund Series** ($n_1=4, 5$): IR

**Selection Rules**: Transitions must satisfy $\Delta n = \text{anything}$, $\Delta l = \pm 1$, $\Delta m_l = 0, \pm 1$.

### Other Elements
Heavier elements have more complex discrete spectra. For example, low-pressure sodium lamps emit a very strong, characteristic yellow doublet around 590 nm.

## Molecular Spectra
When atoms combine into molecules, new quantized modes of energy appear:
1. **Electronic Energy** (largest spacing, ~eV)
2. **Vibrational Energy** (closer spacing, ~0.1 eV)
3. **Rotational Energy** (very close spacing, ~0.01 eV)
4. Translational Energy (nearly continuous)

Because a single electronic transition can be accompanied by multiple vibrational and rotational changes ($\mathcal{E} = \mathcal{E}_{vib} + \mathcal{E}_{rot} + \mathcal{E}_{el}$), molecular spectra contain densely packed "bands" of lines rather than the simple, sparse lines of atomic spectra. These are heavily used in IR spectroscopy for environmental and medical monitoring.
