# High-Energy Photon and Particle Sources

## X-ray Sources
Produced by impacting high-energy electrons onto a solid metal target (like Tungsten) in an evacuated **X-ray tube**. The output spectrum consists of two parts:
1. **Bremsstrahlung (Braking Radiation)**: A continuous spectrum of X-rays produced when incident electrons are deflected and decelerated by the electric field of the target nuclei.
2. **Characteristic X-rays**: Sharp, discrete spectral peaks created when an incident electron ejects a deep inner-shell electron (K, L, M) from a target atom, and a higher-shell electron falls in to fill the vacancy, emitting a specific X-ray photon (e.g., Tungsten peaks at 59 and 67 keV).

## Gamma-ray Sources
Produced naturally by nuclear reactions (fusion, fission, alpha/gamma decay). They have very short wavelengths ($\sim$ Angstroms) and high penetration power.
**Cobalt-60 ($^{60}Co$)**: A common source. It undergoes beta decay to excited Nickel-60, which then relaxes to its ground state by emitting distinct gamma photons at **1.173 MeV** and **1.332 MeV**.
*Applications*: Medical sterilization, oncology radiotherapy, material inspection.

## Alpha Particle Sources
An alpha particle ($^4_2\alpha$) is a Helium nucleus (2 protons, 2 neutrons, net zero spin). 
They are emitted during radioactive decay (e.g., Uranium-238 $\rightarrow$ Thorium-234 + $\alpha$).
- **Properties**: Typically $\sim 5$ MeV energy, travels at $\sim 4\%$ the speed of light. Highly ionizing but low penetration (stopped by paper or a few cm of air).
- **Application**: Used in household smoke detectors (e.g., Americium-241). They ionize air in a chamber to create a small current; smoke entering the chamber blocks the heavy alpha particles, dropping the current and triggering the alarm.
