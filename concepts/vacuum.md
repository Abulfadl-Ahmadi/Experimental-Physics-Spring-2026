# Vacuum Physics and Applications

A **vacuum** is defined as an environment with pressure less than 1 Atmosphere (1 Atm = 10⁵ Pa = 10³ mbar = 760 Torr). Below 10⁻³ Torr, there are more gas molecules on the surface of the vessel than in the volume of the vessel. 

## Pressure Unit Conversions
- **1 atmosphere** = 1.0133 bar = 760 torr = 1.0133 x 10⁵ Pa
- **1 torr** = 1 mm Hg = 133.32 Pa
- **1 millibar** = 100 Pa = 0.75 Torr

## Vacuum Ranges
| Vacuum Region | Pressure Range (Pa) | Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Rough | 10⁵ – 10² | Viscous flow |
| Intermediate | 10² – 10⁻¹ | Changing to molecular |
| High | 10⁻¹ – 10⁻⁴ | Mainly molecular |
| Good high | 10⁻⁴ – 10⁻⁷ | Molecular, Materials tightness, baking (150 – 400 °C) |
| Ultra (UHV) | 10⁻⁷ – 10⁻¹⁰ | Surfaces stay clean, metal seals, baking |

## Force Calculation
Due to atmospheric pressure outside, vacuum chambers experience significant crushing forces. 
$$F = P \cdot A$$
A 10cm x 10cm window experiences a force of roughly 1000 Newtons (equivalent to 100kg of weight).

## Engineering Requirements
Achieving high/ultra vacuums requires:
- Specific [[vacuum-materials]] (to avoid outgassing).
- Metal seals instead of rubber O-rings.
- Baking the chamber at 150–400°C to release adsorbed gases from walls.
