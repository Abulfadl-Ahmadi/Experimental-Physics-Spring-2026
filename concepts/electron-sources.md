# Electron Sources

Electrons are charged particles with mass and spin. In experimental setups, they are generated and formed into beams using an **Electron Gun**. Because they exhibit wave-particle duality, accelerated electrons have a de Broglie wavelength ($\lambda = h/p$) often in the picometer/Angstrom range, making them useful for microscopy.

## Emission Mechanisms
Extracting electrons from a metal (overcoming the Work Function $\Phi$) is achieved primarily via two methods:
1. **Thermionic Emission**: A metal filament (e.g., Tungsten) or oxide crystal is heated to extremely high temperatures until thermal energy exceeds the work function, causing electrons to "boil" off.
2. **Field Emission**: A very sharp metal tip is subjected to an enormous external electric field. This electric field narrows the potential barrier at the surface of the metal, allowing electrons to escape via quantum tunneling even at lower temperatures.

## Electron Gun Structure
Once extracted, electrons must be guided. A standard electron gun consists of:
- **Cathode**: The source of electrons (heated filament or sharp tip).
- **Wehnelt Cylinder**: A grid/control electrode that focuses the initial cloud of electrons.
- **Anodes**: Positively charged plates that accelerate the electrons to high velocities (e.g., 100 kV - 1000 kV).
- **Electric/Magnetic Lenses**: Deflection plates (X and Y) or coils used to focus and steer the beam.

*Applications*: Electron microscopes (SEM/TEM), cathode-ray tubes (old TVs, oscilloscopes), and electron spectroscopy.
