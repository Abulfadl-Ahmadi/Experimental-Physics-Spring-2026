# Gas Liquefaction

Liquefying gases relies heavily on compressing, pre-cooling, and expanding gases iteratively (often using the [[joule-thomson-effect]]).

## Liquid Nitrogen (Linde Method)
Developed by Carl von Linde, this method uses fractional distillation to produce 99.999% pure nitrogen from air.
1. Ambient air is filtered, compressed, and scrubbed of $CO_2$ and water vapor.
2. The compressed air is pre-cooled via heat exchangers and expanded through turbines until it liquefies.
3. The liquid air enters a fractionating column. Nitrogen boils at -196°C and exits from the top as a gas, while Oxygen boils at -183°C and collects as a liquid at the bottom.

## Liquid Hydrogen
Hydrogen must be cooled below its inversion temperature (204 K) before JT expansion can work.
1. Hydrogen gas is compressed and pre-cooled using Liquid Nitrogen (77 K).
2. It is further cooled using cryogenic Helium.
3. Finally, it expands near its boiling point (-253°C) where a portion condenses into Liquid Hydrogen (LH$_2$), heavily used as rocket fuel.

## Liquid Helium (Hampson-Linde Cycle)
Helium has an inversion temperature of 40 K.
1. Helium gas is compressed and pre-cooled using Liquid Nitrogen (down to 77 K), then passed through a counter-flow heat exchanger cooled by returning helium gas.
2. It passes through an expansion valve.
3. The liquefied portion (at 4.2 K) is collected.
4. The remaining cold, low-pressure gas flows back through the heat exchanger to pre-cool the incoming high-pressure gas before returning to the compressor.
