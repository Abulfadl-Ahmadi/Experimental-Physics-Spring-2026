# Cryogenics and Low-Temperature Technology

Cryogenics involves the production and behavior of materials at very low temperatures.

## Applications
- Studying physical properties like superconductivity and the quantum Hall effect (which require eliminating thermal noise).
- Creating ultra-high vacuums.
- Medical and biological preservation (cryopreservation, cryotherapy).
- Food preservation.
- Submarine and rocket fuels (e.g., liquid hydrogen and oxygen).
- Operating strong electromagnets for MRI or magnetic levitation.

## General Cooling Methods
1. Thermal contact with cold solids/liquids (cryogens).
2. Refrigerators using closed cycles and phase changes (e.g., [[joule-thomson-effect]], Stirling cycle).
3. Utilizing phase boundaries via pressure control (e.g., pumping on liquids to lower boiling points).
4. Thermoelectric (Peltier) cooling (see [[non-traditional-cooling]]).
5. Laser cooling and magnetic cooling (see [[millikelvin-cooling]], [[non-traditional-cooling]]).

## Properties of Common Cryogens
A standard $P-T$ Phase Diagram defines the Solid, Liquid, and Gas regions for a substance, meeting at the Triple Point (TP) and ending at the Critical Point (CP).

| Cryogen | Triple Point (K) | Normal Boiling Point (K) | Critical Point (K) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Nitrogen** | 63.1 | 77.3 | 126.2 |
| **Oxygen** | 54.4 | 90.2 | 154.6 |
| **Hydrogen** | 13.8 | 20.4 | 33.2 |
| **Helium-4** | 2.2 ($\lambda$ point) | 4.2 | 5.2 |

*(Note: Helium-4 does not have a standard solid-liquid-gas triple point at ambient pressures. The 2.2K point is the lambda transition into [[superfluidity]]).*
