# Light-Emitting Diodes (LEDs)

LEDs use a thin layer of doped semiconductor (a P-N junction) to convert electrical current directly into light via **spontaneous emission** (unlike [[lasers]] which use stimulated emission).

## Mechanism
When the diode is forward-biased, electrons from the conduction band (N-type) recombine with holes in the valence band (P-type). The energy difference across the semiconductor bandgap ($E_g$) is released as a photon.
Because the layer is very thin, photons can escape the junction to produce light.

## Wavelengths and Materials
The color of the light depends entirely on the bandgap energy of the semiconductor material.
- **GaAs**: 850-940 nm (Infrared)
- **GaAsP**: 630-660 nm (Red) / 585-595 nm (Yellow)
- **AlGaP**: 550-570 nm (Green)
- **SiC**: 430-505 nm (Blue)

## Creating White Light
To generate white light (which requires a broad continuous spectrum), a blue LED (e.g., GaInN) is typically coated with a phosphor layer. The LED emits sharp blue light, and the phosphor absorbs some of this to fluoresce a broad spectrum of yellow/green/red, which mixes with the remaining blue to appear white.
