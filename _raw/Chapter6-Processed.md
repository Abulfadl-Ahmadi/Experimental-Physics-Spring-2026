## Topic 2: Historical Background

### Magdeburg Hemispheres Experiment (1654)
- The Magdeburg hemispheres are a pair of large copper hemispheres, with mating rims. They were used to demonstrate the power of atmospheric pressure.
- When the rims were sealed with grease and the air was pumped out, the sphere contained a vacuum and could not be pulled apart by teams of horses in a famous first public demonstration in 1654.
- Force of around 20,000 newtons equivalent to lifting a car or small elephant; a dramatic demonstration of the pressure of the atmosphere.
- Image caption: "Die Magdeburger Halbkugeln auf dem Reichstag zu Regensburg, 1654."
- Showcases Otto von Guericke's pivotal 1654 experiment which proved that atmospheric pressure exerts immense force.

## Topic 6: Gas Flow Regimes

### Knudsen Number Definition
- Knudsen number: λ/D = Kn
- The flow dynamics is characterized by the comparison of the mean free path λ to the dimension D of the vacuum vessel.

### Flow Regime Classification
| Flow Type | Condition | Knudsen Range |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Free Molecular Flow | λ > D | Kn > 1 |
| Transitional (or intermediate) flow | D/100 < λ < D | 0.01 < Kn < 1 |
| Viscous (continuum) flow | λ < D/100 | Kn < 0.01 |

### Flow Regime Characteristics
- **Viscous flow:** Kn = λ/a < 0.01. Dominated by collisions between molecules.
- **Transition flow:** 0.01 < Kn < 1.0
- **Molecular flow:** Kn > 1.0. The molecules collide with a wall at high frequencies.
- Viscous flow (Kn < 0.01): Molecules collide with each other; behaves like a fluid.
- Molecular flow (Kn > 1.0): Molecules bounce off the walls of the chamber at high frequencies, changing how pumping speed is calculated.

### Practical Rules (for N₂ at Room Temperature)
- Molecular flow: p · D < 0.064 [mbar · mm]
- Viscous flow: p · D > 6.4 [mbar · mm]

## Topic 9: Gas Constants & Equations of State

### Fundamental Constants
- N_A = 6.02205 · 10²³ mol⁻¹ — Avogadro constant
- 1 mol ideal gas in NTP = 22.4 l
- R = 8.3144 J/mol·K — molar gas constant
- k = R/N_A = 1.3806 · 10⁻²³ J/K — Boltzmann constant

### Ideal Gas Equations
- P · V = n · R · T — Equation of state for residual gas in terms of moles
- P · V = N · k · T — Equation of state for residual gas in terms of number of molecules

### Context for Usage
- In the laboratory, we mostly deal with the molecular regime. The approximation of ideal gases is good.
- When dealing with Molecular flow, the Ideal Gas approximation is still useful.

## Topic 10: Molecular Density Calculations

### Molecules in Normal Conditions
- In NTP: N = (P·V)/(k·T) = (10⁵ N/m² · 10⁻⁶ m³)/(1.38 · 10⁻²³ J/K · 293K) = 2.5 · 10¹⁹ molecules in 1 cm³
- In normal air, a 1cm cube contains 25 quintillion (2.5 × 10¹⁹) molecules!

### Molecules in Vacuum
- In 10⁻⁷ mbar = 10⁻⁵ Pa vacuum: N = 2.5 × 10⁹ molecules in cm³
- Even after pumping down to a 10⁻⁷ mbar "good high" vacuum, there are still 2.5 billion (2.5 × 10⁹) molecules in that same tiny cube.

## Topic 11: Molecular Impact Rate

### Impact Rate Formula
- Number of impacts per unit time per unit wall surface = n·v̄_z
- = n ∫₀^+∞ f_z v_z dv_z = n (kT/2πm)^(1/2)
- Using ideal gas law, n = P/kT
- Final Boxed Formula: z = P/(2πm k T)^(1/2)

### Significance
- This equation is crucial in vacuum science because it dictates how fast gas molecules will hit the walls of a chamber (and thus, the rate of gas load or surface contamination).
- By substituting the ideal gas law, the final equation relates this impact rate directly to the pressure (P) inside the chamber.

## Topic 12: Monolayer Formation Time

### Formulas
- Collision rate: Φ = P/(2πm k T)^(1/2)
- Time for one molecular layer formation: τ_m = 1/(ξ²Φ) (where ξ = residual gas molecule diameter)

### Practical Calculation (Hydrogen at UHV)
- At pressure of 10⁻⁵ Pa and at 20°C:
- τ_m = (2π · 2.016 · 1.66 · 10⁻²⁷ kg · 1.38 · 10⁻²³ J/K · 293 K)^(1/2) / ((2.68 · 10⁻¹⁰ m)² · 10⁻⁵ N/m) = 12.8 s

### Significance
- Even at a very low pressure of 10⁻⁵ Pa, it only takes 12.8 seconds for a monolayer of Hydrogen molecules to form on a completely clean surface.
- This is why UHV systems must be meticulously cleaned and baked: to prevent contaminants from rapidly re-covering experimental surfaces.

## Topic 13: Mean Free Path

### Definition & Derivation
- Mean free path for air at 20 °C: λ = 7 × 10⁻⁵ m / P(mbar)
- At pressure 1 bar: λ = 70 nm only
- Cross-sectional area: A = πd²
- Volume = πd² v̄ t
- Mean free path estimate = (Distance traveled)/(Volume of interaction) = v̄t/(πd² v̄t n_v) = 1/(πd² n_v)
- Mean free path (λ) is inversely proportional to the cross-sectional area of a molecule (πd²) and the number density (n_v).

### Inverse Pressure Relationship
- Formula: p · λ = kT/(√2 π d_mol²)
- For N₂: d_mol = 0.31 nm = 0.31 × 10⁻⁹ m

### Collision Distance vs. Pressure Table (N₂ at 20°C)
| Pressure [Pa] | Collision distance |
| :--- | :--- |
| 10⁻¹⁰ | 64000 km |
| 10⁻⁷ | 64 km |
| 10⁻⁴ | 64 m |
| 10⁻¹ | 64 mm |
| 10² | 64 μm |
| 10⁵ | 64 nm |

### Significance
- At an ultra-high vacuum of 10⁻¹⁰ Pa, a molecule will travel 64,000 kilometers before hitting another molecule (roughly double the circumference of the Earth).

## Topic 14: Velocity Distribution (Maxwell-Boltzmann)

### Distribution Function
- dN/dv = 4πN (m/(2πkT))^(3/2) v² e^(-mv²/2kT)

### Key Velocities
- Average velocity: ⟨v⟩ = (8kT/πm)^(1/2)
- Most probable velocity: v_prob = (2kT/m)^(1/2)
- Root-mean-square speed: v_rms = √(3kT/m)

### Velocity Relationships
- Most probable speed is the slowest of the three, while Root-mean-square speed is the fastest.
- Lighter gases or higher temperatures shift the curve to the right (faster speeds), meaning they are harder to trap in a vacuum chamber.

### Nitrogen Velocity Distribution at Different Temperatures
- T = -196 °C ; ⟨v⟩ = 242 m/s (tall and narrow curve)
- T = 20 °C ; ⟨v⟩ = 471 m/s
- T = 1000 °C ; ⟨v⟩ = 981 m/s (wide and flat curve)

### Connection to Vacuum Engineering
- This explains why we "bake" vacuum chambers. High temperatures give adsorbed molecules enough kinetic energy to break free from the walls into the gas phase so they can be pumped away.

## Topic 16: Pumping Speed & Throughput

### Fundamental Definitions
- Pumping speed / Volumetric flow rate: S = dV/dt
- Transmission of the pump: Q = P · S
- Throughput = Gas flow rate / Quantity of gas: Q = d(pV)/dt = dN/dt (k_B T) = dN_mol/dt RT

### Term Clarifications
- dN/dt → particle flow rate
- k_B T → packet of energy

### Key Distinction
- Speed (S) is purely volumetric (l/s). Throughput (Q) is pressure × volume per second (measured in mbar·l/s), which represents the actual quantity/mass of gas molecules moving per second.
- Q is constant throughout a pumping system (if temperature is constant).

## Topic 17: Conductance

### Basic Definition
- Q = C · (P₁ - P₂)
- C represents the flow conductance of material and depends on the channel's geometry.
- Unit: [C] = m³/s

### Analogy to Electricity
- Conductance in vacuum is directly analogous to electrical conductance. Q is like electrical current, C is like conductance (1/Resistance), and (P₁ - P₂) is like voltage difference. The wider and shorter the pipe, the higher the C.

### Conductance of an Aperture (Molecular Flow)
- Derivation: Q = √(RT/(2πm)) · A(p₁ - p₂)
- C_A = A √(RT/(2πm)) ∝ √(T/m)
- For air at 20°C: C_A = 11.8 · A [l·s⁻¹] with A in cm²

### Series Connection
- Formula: 1/C_TOTAL = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃ ...
- In series, the smallest conductance (C) largely dictates the total conductance.

### Parallel Connection
- Formula: C_TOTAL = C₁ + C₂ + C₃ ...
- In parallel, the total conductance is simply the sum of the individual conductances.

## Topic 18: Effective Pumping Speed

### The Critical Formula
- 1/S_eff = 1/C + 1/S_nom

### Conservation of Mass Demonstration
- At Chamber: Pressure P = 1 × 10⁻⁵ mbar | Pumping Speed S = 2 l/s
- Middle Pipe: Pressure P = 1 × 10⁻⁶ mbar | Pumping Speed S = 20 l/s
- At Pump: Pressure P = 1 × 10⁻⁷ mbar | Pumping Speed S = 200 l/s
- Throughput Q remains constant at 2 × 10⁻⁵ mbar·l/s (Since Q = S · P).

### Effect of Small Orifice
- Pump A (Speed = 300 l/s, opening 1 cm²): S_c = (300 × 11.6)/(300 + 11.6) = 11.21 l/s
- Pump B (Speed = 30,000 l/s, opening 1 cm²): S_c = (30000 × 11.6)/(30000 + 11.6) = 11.61 l/s
- Even with a pump that is 100 times faster, the effective pumping speed only increased negligibly. The conductance of the pipe dictates the maximum effective speed you can ever achieve.

## Topic 19: Gas Loads in Vacuum Systems

### Total Gas Load Equation
- Q_tot = Q_G + Q_L + Q_p + etc.

### Sources of Gas
- Permeation (from outside air through walls)
- Real leak (actual holes to outside)
- Desorption (e.g. thermal release from internal walls)
- Virtual leak (gas trapped in internal crevices that slowly escapes)
- Residual gas (gas left in the chamber)
- Back-streaming (from the pump back into the chamber)
- Evaporation (from materials inside the vacuum)
- Bulk diffusion (gas escaping from deep inside the metal walls)

### Ultimate Pressure Limit
- The final lowest pressure you can reach (Ultimate Pressure) is directly dictated by the sum of all these gas loads (Q_tot) divided by your pumping speed (S).

## Topic 20: Pumping Equations & Pumpdown

### Basic Pumping Equation (Continuity Equation)
- V · dp = Q_tot · dt - S · p · dt
- Q_tot · dt = Amount of gas getting into the free volume in a time dt at pressure p and speed S
- S · p · dt = Gas pumped away in a time dt at pressure p and speed S
- V · dp = Change of amount of free gas in the volume V

### Rearranged Form
- -V(dp/dt) = S · p - Q_tot
- The rate of change of the amount of gas in a chamber is the difference between the rate of its removal and the influx rate.

### Initial Phase Pumpdown (Ignoring Gas Loads)
- V(dp/dt) = -S p
- Solution: p_initial = p₀ e^(-S/V · t) = p₀ e^(-t/τ)
- τ = V/S is the Characteristic time or time constant
- After 1τ of pumping, the pressure will drop to 37% of its starting value. After 5τ, it drops to less than 1%.

### Time Calculation
- t = (V/S) ln(p₀/p)
- Time per decade = τ ln(10) = 2.3τ

### Example Calculation
- A 50 l volume pumped with S = 1 l/s from 1000 mbar to 1 mbar:
  - τ = V/S = 50 s
  - Time per decade = τ ln(10) = 115 s
  - Total time = 3 · 115 s = 345 s (Since 1000 to 1 is 3 decades)

## Topic 21: Outgassing Effects on Pumpdown

### Deviation from Ideal Behavior
- Below 1 Pa = 10⁻² mbar, (~roughing), the curve p(t) starts to deviate from the "free volume" straight line. We cannot neglect outgassing Q_out anymore.

### Outgassing Phase Equation
- p_ultimate = k t^(-n) with 0.5 ≤ n ≤ 1.2
- n ~ 1 for metallic unbaked surfaces
- n ~ 0.5 for elastomers, (for baked metallic surfaces)

### Ultimate Pressure Limit
- When pressure ceases to fall and becomes constant: dp/dt = 0
- Ultimate Pressure Formula: p_ultimate = Q_out / S
- To get a lower ultimate pressure, you have two options:
  1. Increase the pumping speed (S).
  2. Decrease the outgassing/leakage rate (Q_out) (which is why baking and proper metal seals are used in UHV).

### Pumpdown with Pump Ultimate Limit (P_s)
- t = (C/S) ln((P₀ - P_s)/(P_t - P_s))
- P_t = P_s + (P₀ - P_s)e^(-St/C)

### Practical Pumpdown Time Calculation
- Assumptions:
  1. Path resistance is ignored.
  2. No leakage from outside into the chamber.
  3. No internal source of gas generation.
  4. Vapor pressure of materials inside is much lower than final pressure.
  5. Pump speed is constant in the pressure range.
- Example: V = 500 lit, t = 6 min, P₀ = 1 atm = 1013 mbar, P₁ = 1 mbar
  - S_eff = (V/t) ln(P₀/P₁) = (0.5/0.1) ln(1013/1) = 34.6 m³/hr

### Full Integration with Conductance
- 1/S_s = 1/S_p + 1/C₀
- t_p = (V/S_s) ln((p₁ - p_u)/(p₂ - p_u))
- As your target pressure p₂ gets closer to the ultimate pressure p_u, the logarithm value blows up towards infinity, meaning it takes exponentially longer to get those last few mTorr out.

## Topic 22: Classification of Vacuum Pumps

### Two Main Families
- **Gas Transfer** pumps physically push molecules out.
- **Entrapment** pumps capture molecules and hold them inside the pump.

### Hierarchy
- **Gas Transfer** → **Positive Displacement**: Sliding Vane, Rotary, Roots
- **Gas Transfer** → **Kinetic**: Diffusion, Turbo Molecular, Fluid Entrainment
- **Entrapment** → Absorption, Cryo Pump, Getter

## Topic 23: Mechanical Pumps

### Rotary Vane Pumps
- An oil seal between a phenolic vane and a steel cylinder is used to remove gas from the vacuum region and exhaust it to the atmosphere.
- This pump works from atmosphere to about 0.1 mTorr.
- Lowest pressure: ca 10⁻³ torr
- Precautions must be taken at low pressures to avoid oil backstreaming into the vacuum vessel.
- It is also used as a backing pump for compression pumps like a diffusion pump or turbomolecular pump.

### Operating Cycle (Rotary Vane)
- 1. Induction (Gas is drawn in as the vane sweeps)
- 2. Isolation (Gas is trapped between the vane and the wall)
- 3. Compression (Space shrinks, compressing the gas)
- 4. Exhaust (Compressed gas is pushed out through the exhaust valve)

### Roots Pumps
- The two figure-8 lobes rotate, trapping gas between them and the housing, moving it from the high-vacuum inlet side to the low-vacuum exhaust side without internal compression.
- A Roots pump does not compress the gas internally; it simply moves the gas from one side to the other.
- Roots pumps cannot exhaust gas directly to atmosphere; they must have a backing pump.

### General Characteristics of Mechanical Pumps
- Have moving parts (one or more rotors inside the stator body) that compress the gas and send it out by opening a passage.
- To prevent gas backflow, the body is usually coated with oil, and the distance between the rotating parts and the body must be very small.
- To avoid body abrasion, large particles must not enter. Having a mesh at the inlet of mechanical pumps is mandatory.
- These pumps work well from atmospheric pressure to about one-tenth of a Torr, and in some two-stage types, up to one-hundredth of a Torr.

### Cross-Section Components
- Two-stage rotary vane pump: I: High vacuum stage, II: Second forevacuum stage.
- Single-stage rotary plunger pump parts: Casing, Cylindrical piston, Eccentric, Compression chamber, Oil level control valve, Oil level sight glass, Gas ballast valve, Exhaust port, Intake port, Slide valve, Backing pump, Pumping chamber, Oil lift.
- Roots pump parts: Intake flange, Rotors, Chamber, Exhaust flange, Casing.
- Eccentric piston pump parts: Toothed wheel fixed to driving shaft, Toothed wheel fixed to piston, Eccentric piston, Inner surface of pump, Driving shaft, Eccentric.

## Topic 24: Diffusion Pumps

### Operating Principle
- The diffusion pump contains heavy oil that is heated at the bottom by a heater and evaporates upward as vapor streams (like a fountain).
- As gas molecules pass through these fine nozzles, the oil droplets collide with the gas molecules, giving them downward momentum.
- With the help of a mechanical backing pump, these gas molecules are evacuated.
- Upon hitting the pump's walls (which are typically cooled with water), the oil vapor condenses and returns to the bottom to be recirculated.
- Momentum transfer to gas molecules through collision with directed jet of oil molecules.
- Require cooling water, backing pump.

### Critical Accessories
- The "cooling water" cools the walls to recondense the oil.
- The "Cold Trap" (usually cooled by liquid nitrogen) prevents the oil vapor from rising and contaminating the UHV chamber above.
- (a) The water-cooled circular chevron baffle.
- (b) The liquid nitrogen cold trap.

### Components
- Pipe (cooling water), Jet, Inlet, Exhaust, Vacuum oil, Heater.
- The central "chimney" structure has multiple stacked nozzles, spraying oil jets downward and outward in stages.

## Topic 25: Turbomolecular Pumps

### Operating Principle
- Turbomolecular pumps have a structure like a jet engine. They have a series of rotating blades (rotor) and fixed blades (stator).
- High-speed rotation causes gas molecules to collide with the rotating blades, gaining momentum that directs them outwards.
- The stator blades deflect the molecules further, ensuring they don't come back inwards.
- They rely purely on mechanical momentum transfer.

### Design Features
- Turbomolecular pumps have very high rotation speeds and very tight gaps between the blades and the body to minimize backflow.
- They do not operate well at atmospheric pressure (molecules are too large and heavy) and require a small mesh or filter at the inlet to prevent debris from entering and damaging the blades.
- Because of the incredibly high blade speeds (up to 90,000 RPM), they can handle heavy gas molecules, but they must be backed by a roughing pump because they cannot exhaust directly to atmosphere.

### Components
- Magnetic bearing, High vacuum port, Rotor, Stator, Pump body, Vent, Purge gas line, Electronics, Motor, Ceramic bearing, Backing port/path.

## Topic 26: Ion Pumps (Getter/Sputter)

### Operating Principle
- This pump operates on the principle of trapping gas particles.
- A strong electric field and magnetic field are applied. Inside the pump, electrons fly at high speeds, colliding with and ionizing the gas molecules.
- These ionized particles are accelerated towards the Titanium cathode plates. The impact causes Titanium atoms to be sputtered (knocked off) onto the walls of the pump.
- The active Titanium chemically reacts with (gets) active gases like Oxygen and Nitrogen, burying them into the pump's body.
- Noble gases (like Argon) are usually trapped slightly differently, by being buried just below the surface.
- Because of the magnetic field, the electrons travel extremely long helical paths, increasing the chance of ionization.

### Configuration
- Diagram of an ion pump: Ion pump configuration, Titanium cathode, Anode with honeycomb structure (anode array), Magnet, Titanium cathode, N-pole, S-pole.
- Legend: Electron, Titanium atom, Gas molecules, Gas+ions.

### Key Characteristics
- Ion pumps are "Entrapment" pumps. They trap gases inside the pump itself by chemical reaction (chemisorption).
- Once they pump down to a good vacuum, they can be turned off and will maintain the vacuum for a long time because they don't have moving parts and don't need oil.
- The pump achieves very low ultimate pressures (microTorr to less).

### Important Warning
- Ion pumps must never be turned on at atmospheric pressure! The high density of gas would cause rapid sputtering and quickly burn through the fresh Titanium target, destroying the pump.

## Topic 27: Cryopumps

### Operating Principle
- The working principle of these pumps is the use of extremely cold surfaces inside the pump chamber.
- At temperatures below 80 Kelvin, they easily trap Oxygen and Nitrogen molecules.
- To trap atoms and molecules like Hydrogen and Helium, liquid helium is used.

### Components & Temperature Stages
- Inlet Baffle (which captures water vapor)
- First Stage Array (cools to ~70-80K to trap H₂O, CO₂)
- Second Stage Array (cools to ~10-20K to trap N₂, O₂, Ar)
- Refrigerator

### Key Characteristics
- Cryopumps are completely oil-free, making them ideal for semiconductor manufacturing.
- Because these pumps have no moving parts, they are very suitable for epitaxy and multi-layer thin-film deposition as well as microelectronics manufacturing equipment.
- Their ultimate pressure is in the range of one microTorr or less.
- They cannot pump Hydrogen or Helium very effectively unless cooled with extremely expensive liquid Helium.

## Topic 28: Vapor Pressure Curves of Gases

### Graph Description
- A log-linear plot of Vapor pressure (mbar) vs Temperature (K) for He, H₂, Ne, N₂, O₂, CO₂, H₂O.
- Y-axis: vapor pressure, mbar (10³, 1, 10⁻³, 10⁻⁶, 10⁻⁹). X-axis: temperature, K (3, 5, 10, 20, 40, 100, 200, 300).

### Freezing Principle
- At 20 Kelvin (in a cryopump's second stage), the vapor pressure of N₂, O₂, CO₂, and H₂O drops below 10⁻⁹ mbar, meaning they are effectively frozen solid and removed from the gas phase.

### Helium Problem
- Helium (He) remains a gas even at 3 Kelvin. This is why Cryopumps alone cannot achieve true UHV without an additional pump (like an Ion pump) to deal with Helium and Hydrogen.

## Topic 30: Pressure Measurement - Manometers

### U-Tube Manometer
- The simplest gauge is the mercury type, measuring the height difference of the mercury column.
- Sensitivity = h/(p - p_a) = h/Δp = 1/((g/g_c)(ρ_m - ρ_f))
- For a manometer with ρ_m ≫ ρ_f, Sensitivity = 1/(ρ_m(g/g_c))
- Pressure Balance: p_a + (g/g_c)hρ_m = p + (g/g_c)hρ_f
- Or p - p_a = (g/g_c)h(ρ_m - ρ_f)
- The pressure difference depends directly on the difference in density between the manometer fluid (ρ_m) and the fluid/gas being measured (ρ_f).

### Well-Type Manometer
- Geometric Equation: h'A₂ = (h - h')A₁
- Pressure Equation: p - p_a = (g/g_c)h'(A₂/A₁ + 1)(ρ_m - ρ_f)
- Example area ratio: A₂/A₁ = 0.03
- By making the reservoir extremely large (A₁), the drop in its fluid level when the measuring tube (A₂) rises becomes negligible.
- Simplified when A₂/A₁ is negligible: P₁ - P₂ = ρ · g · h

### Barometer & Atmospheric Pressure
- p (absolute) = p (gage) + p (atm)
- Altitude Equation: p = p₀ (1 - BZ/T₀)^5.26
- p₀ = standard atmospheric pressure at sea level
- Z = altitude, m or ft
- T₀ = 518.69 °R = 288.16 K = 15 °C
- B = 0.003566 °R/ft = 0.00650 K/m

### U-Tube Calculation Example
- Problem: Oil specific gravity 0.82, local atmospheric pressure 29.3 inHg, column height difference 20 cm ± 1.0 mm at 25°C.
- ρ_m = 0.82ρ_w = (0.82)(996 kg/m³) = 816.7 kg/m³
- p_a = 29.3 inHg = 9.922 × 10⁴ Pa
- ρ_f = ρ_a = p/(RT) = 9.922 × 10⁴/((287)(298)) = 1.16 kg/m³
- p - p_a = (g/g_c)h(ρ_m - ρ_f) = (9.807/1.0)(0.2)(816.7 - 1.16) = 1600 Pa
- p = 1600 + 9.922 × 10⁴ = 1.0082 × 10⁵ Pa

## Topic 31: Pressure Measurement - McLeod Gauge

### Operating Principle
- Accuracy up to fractions of a millimeter of mercury.
- First, by leveling the gauge, the capillary tube connects with the chamber, then it returns to its initial state and the mercury level...
- Components: To vacuum space (p), Reference (a zero line), Capillary (C), Bulb (B), Opening (O), Movable reservoir.

### Key Characteristic
- The McLeod gauge is an absolute gauge. It does not need calibration because it works purely by physically compressing a known volume of gas (from the Bulb B into the Capillary C) and measuring the resulting pressure via the height difference in the capillary.
- It was the historical gold standard for measuring very low pressures (below 10⁻³ Torr) before electronic gauges existed.

## Topic 32: Pressure Measurement - Pirani Gauge

### Operating Principle
- Works on the principle of placing a heated filament in a vacuum system.
- A hot wire is heated with constant current. The rate at which it loses heat depends on the number of gas molecules colliding with it—which is proportional to pressure.
- Higher pressure = more heat removed = cooler wire = lower resistance.

### Temperature Compensation
- Figure 6.17 shows a Wheatstone bridge circuit with resistors R₁, R₂, R₃ and two similar filament bulbs, one in vacuum, one sealed/evacuated as a reference.
- A second, sealed Pirani gauge acts as a "reference" to cancel out environmental temperature changes.

## Topic 33: Pressure Measurement - Ionization Gauge

### Operating Principle
- Ion gauge is a standard measurement of ion current which depends on the number of particles, i.e., pressure.
- To ionize, we use either an electric field with a hot filament, or a radioactive source.
- Primary Equation: p = (1/S)(i_p/i_g) (S is the gauge's sensitivity, i_p is plate/collector current, i_g is grid/emission current).

### Hot Filament Ionization Gauge
- The filament emits electrons. The presence of the electric field causes them to accelerate and collide with the atoms and molecules of the gas, ionizing them.
- Collecting these ions and measuring the resulting ion current can serve as a standard for the absolute pressure.
- Schematic shows: Grid, Plate, Cathode, Vacuum source, and two power supplies E_p, E_g.

### Alphatron Gauge
- Uses a radioactive source (Radium) to ionize the gas instead of a hot filament.
- Schematic shows: Radium source enclosed, Ion collector, and a resistor R with an applied voltage.

### Critical Warning
- This ionization gauge must only be turned on at low pressures (vacuum). If turned on at atmospheric pressure, the high density of gas will cause the filament to burn out and the gauge will be permanently damaged.
- Gauges are typically interlocked to only turn on when a Pirani gauge confirms the pressure is below 10⁻⁴ Torr.

## Topic 34: Pressure Gauge Ranges

### Applicable Pressure Ranges of Gauges (from highest to lowest pressure)
- Electric resistance (Up to 10⁹ Pa, from atmospheric upwards)
- Bourdon
- Diaphragm
- Bellows
- Piezoelectric
- Manometers
- McLeod (covers high vacuum down to 10⁻⁶ Pa)
- Pirani (covers high vacuum down to ~10⁻⁴ Pa)
- Ionization (covers Ultra-High Vacuum, from ~10⁻² down to 10⁻¹⁰ Pa and below)

### Key Insight
- No single gauge can measure the entire pressure range from atmosphere to UHV. You must use multiple gauges (a Pirani for roughing to high vacuum, and an Ion gauge for UHV) in sequence to accurately monitor a complete pumpdown.

## Topic 35: Vacuum Compatible Materials

### Requirements for Vacuum Materials
- Materials must have low vapor pressure; metals are stable in vacuum, but polymers and liquids are not durable.
- Components must be compatible regarding thermal expansion and contraction to prevent gaps at connections.
- Must be clean and have minimal outgassing, be chemically stable inside the vacuum, and not react with the environment.
- Must not generate gas when exposed to high-energy particles or photons (relevant for particle accelerators).

### Recommended Materials
- **Stainless Steel:** Strong, low outgassing, smooth surface, pinhole-free, and low chemical reactivity.
- **Aluminum:** Easy machining, moderate strength, and moderate corrosion resistance.
- **Kovar (Nickel alloy):** Good mechanical strength at high temperatures, but costly and difficult to machine.
- **Copper:** Good corrosion resistance, good thermal and electrical conductivity, easy to machine, and does not absorb Oxygen.

### Materials to Avoid in UHV
- **Plastics and Polymers:** High vapor pressure and high gas adsorption/permeability; they are only usable in medium vacuum applications as washers or seals.
- Plastics and rubber are the enemy of UHV because they trap water and release it slowly, making it impossible to reach ultra-low pressures.

## Topic 36: High-Vacuum System Configuration

### Conventional High-Vacuum System Components
- Roughing pump
- Diffusion pump (or Turbomolecular pump as Secondary pump)
- Cold trap
- Chamber to be evacuated
- Thermocouple (TC) gauge #1
- Thermocouple gauge (TC) #2
- Ionization gauge

### Valve Configuration
- Gauge, Roughing valve, Vacuum chamber, Vent valve, Gate valve, Secondary pump, Backing (foreline) valve, Roughing pump.

### Operational Sequencing
- Operating a high-vacuum system requires precise valve sequencing.
- You must never open the Gate valve (connecting the chamber to the Secondary pump) until the secondary pump has been roughed down to its operating pressure (usually < 10⁻² Torr) by the Roughing pump.
- You must always close the Gate valve before venting the chamber to atmosphere to prevent backstreaming oil or exposing the turbopump to atmosphere.
